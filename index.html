Who is Alex, really?

Alex is that friend everyone has: the one who is weirdly talented at a lot of things, has a story for every failure, and somehow turns every almost-success into a personality trait.

He has never worked an office job a single day in his life – not one. Instead, he has built a heroic résumé of nearly-professional chaos:

  • Bars: started strong, knew all the cocktails, disappeared halfway through the season.
  • Acting: calls himself an actor, lands roughly two jobs a year and celebrates each like an Oscar.
  • Poker: “almost” went pro, then “almost” went broke.
  • Pool: is still absolutely certain he “could have gone pro” if life hadn’t “intervened.”

The pattern was so consistent that his friends started joking there must be a medical explanation. Unfortunately, Alex heard that and ran with it.

Ultra-Rare Talent Application Myopathy (URTAM)

URTAM is a highly suspicious, completely unofficial condition where the patient’s body violently rejects any attempt to turn talent into sustained employment. It only flares up in very specific situations:

  • When a bar manager says the words “double shift” or “summer season contract”.
  • When an acting job requires waking up before 7:00 AM more than two days in a row.
  • When poker stops being “fun” and starts being “bankroll management”.
  • Whenever someone asks, “So what do you do, like, full time?”

Outside of those triggers, Alex can: laugh, tell stories, line up a perfect pool shot, and charm a whole bar. Inside those triggers, his system collapses into a tragic, beautifully cinematic “I just can’t” montage.

The Four Careers That Almost Happened

1. Alex the Bartender

Behind the bar, Alex is a natural: fast hands, good memory, joke for every customer. Unfortunately his URTAM symptoms spike around week three of the job. Shifts get longer, managers start asking about “availability,” and his nervous system quietly whispers, “What if we simply didn’t show up?”

2. Alex the Actor

Twice a year, the universe remembers he’s an actor. He gets a role, transforms, gives everything on set… then spends the next four months telling everyone: “I’ve been so busy with projects,” while his phone stays suspiciously silent.

3. Alex the Poker Pro (almost)

At one point, Alex seriously tried to go pro at poker. He studied ranges, watched training videos, talked about “EV” and “bankroll”. Then URTAM kicked in: the moment discipline was required, his whole being defaulted to “all-in because I feel it”. Spoiler: the cards did not feel it back.

4. Alex the Pool Phenomenon (allegedly)

According to Alex, he was “this close” to going pro in pool. No one has ever seen actual evidence of tournaments, coaches, or rankings. But every bar with a pool table becomes a TED Talk about “what could have been if life hadn’t gotten in the way.”

Why Alex Cannot Just “Get a Job”

URTAM is not about laziness. If laziness were the problem, he would not have survived brutal bar nights, auditions, or 12-hour poker sessions.

The problem is this: the moment any of those things start to look like an actual long-term plan, his body and brain experience:

  • Shift-Induced Vanishing Reflex – confirmed shifts mysteriously “slip his mind”.
  • Audition Fog – lines memorised perfectly… until the camera is actually rolling.
  • Bankroll Amnesia – remembers every bad beat, forgets every bankroll guideline.
  • “I Could Have Gone Pro” Syndrome – retroactively becomes elite at everything he quit.

The result: no stable job, unpredictable income, and a man who genuinely wants to do something with his life, but somehow keeps ending up back at zero with a great story instead of a payslip.
